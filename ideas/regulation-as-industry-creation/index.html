Regulation as a Job-Creation Instrument: The Untapped Economic Logic

December 2025 10 min read

When governments face high unemployment, the traditional toolkit is predictable: increase public spending, offer tax holidays to foreign firms, or launch vocational training schemes. These interventions, while often well-intentioned, treat employment as a byproduct of expenditure. At Plus Element Solutions, we argue for a more rigorous and durable approach: treating regulation as the primary engine for job creation.

This is not about the "red tape" of the past. It is about the Regulatory Architecture of the Future. Historically, the most durable and high-value professions were not created by market demand alone, but by a regulatory standard that redefined what was "necessary" for the functioning of society.

"Markets create jobs; but Regulation creates Professions. One is temporary; the other is institutional."

The Logic of the "Necessary Profession"

Consider the profession of the Auditor. Before corporate governance regulations mandated independent financial verification, the "Auditor" was an occasional consultant. Once regulation made the audit a legal requirement for market participation, an entire multi-billion dollar industry was born overnight — creating millions of high-paying jobs globally.

This logic can be applied to the emerging challenges of the 21st century. In Nigeria and across Africa, we have massive gaps in safety, quality, and data integrity. By creating high-standard regulatory mandates in these areas, we don't just "fix things"; we create entirely new career paths for thousands of young professionals.

The Standards-to-Employment Pipeline

  • Cybersecurity Compliance: If every firm above a certain size is mandated to have a biannual "Digital Health Audit," you create immediate, mandatory demand for 50,000 certified cybersecurity professionals.
  • Indigenous Content Verification: Creating a regulatory requirement for "Local Value Verification" in the tech industry creates a new class of professional analysts who ensure the local economy is capturing the value it creates.
  • Quality Control Ecosystems: Mandating high-frequency safety standards in the manufacturing sector creates a market for independent verification labs — generating jobs for chemists, engineers, and logistics specialists.

Strategic Insight: The Multiplier Effect

Regulatory job creation is "fiscal positive." Unlike government spending, which drains the treasury, regulatory mandates create private-sector demand. The practitioners in these new fields pay taxes, build skills, and eventually drive innovation back into the economy. The institution creates the "demand" through policy; the market provides the "supply" through talent.

Beyond Low-Value Manual Labor

Too many job-creation programs focus on "low-hanging fruit" — manual labor or micro-entrepreneurship. While these are necessary for subsistence, they don't solve the long-term institutional deficit.

Regulation allows us to create Formal Dignity. When you regulate a craft into a profession (e.g., formalizing the informal maintenance sector through safety certifications), you elevate the dignity of that work. You turn a "worker" into a "licensed practitioner." This shift in status is what brings the informal economy "in from the cold" and increases the overall productivity of the nation.

The Risk of "Regulatory Rent-Seeking"

A warning is necessary: for regulation to create jobs, it must facilitate trust, not friction. If the regulation is merely a way for the state to collect a fee without adding value, it becomes an "extractive cost" that kills jobs.

True Job-Creating Regulation must satisfy three conditions:

  1. Value Addition: The professional service mandated must increase the overall efficiency or safety of the market.
  2. Accessibility: The pathway to certification must be rigorous but merit-based, allowing young talent to enter the field without political connection.
  3. Global Compatibility: The standards set must align with international best practices, allowing our professionals to export their services globally.

Conclusion: The Governor as Architect

The modern governor should not be a "job hunter"; they should be an Institution Architect. By designing high-value regulatory frameworks, we create the "Gravity" that pulls people into productive, formal, and high-value employment.

This is the "Plus Element" approach. We don't wait for the economy to grow; we design the institutional conditions that make growth — and the jobs that come with it — inevitable.
