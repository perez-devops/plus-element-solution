Challenging the Necessity of Tax: Toward a Revenue Architecture That Rewards Prosperity

February 2026 8 min read

The conventional wisdom that taxation is the inevitable price of civilisation deserves scrutiny. For centuries, the relationship between the state and the citizen has been defined by fiscal extraction — a zero-sum calculation where a percentage of private output is surrendered to the public treasury in exchange for order, infrastructure, and basic services. But as we navigate a world of rapid digital transformation, decentralized value creation, and institutional decay, we must ask: Is taxation the only way to finance a modern economy? Or is it merely a relic of a pre-digital age?

At Plus Element Solutions, our core operating philosophy centers on "Institutional Gravity." We argue that the current global fiscal model often act as a friction on the very prosperity it seeks to fund. When we tax income, we effectively tax effort. When we tax profit, we tax success. This creates a fundamental paradox where the state becomes a silent partner in every enterprise, capturing value without necessarily contributing to the quality of its creation.

"The state should not be a bounty hunter tracking down private wealth; it should be a platform provider earning its revenue through the sheer utility of its existence."

The Extraction Paradox in Emerging Economies

In economies like Nigeria, the "tax-to-GDP" ratio is often cited as a critical metric for development. The common prescription is simple: expand the net, increase enforcement, and capture more value from the informal sector. However, this approach ignores the Social Contract Gap. When citizens provide their own security, their own electricity, and their own infrastructure, taxation is viewed not as a contribution, but as a penalty for being visible.

The result is a system of evasive productivity. Capital stays small, stays informal, and stays hidden to avoid the extractive reach of an institution that provides little in return. To break this cycle, we must move from a "Revenue through Seizure" model to a "Revenue through Service" model.

The Regulatory Alternative: Standards as Revenue

What if regulatory design, rather than fiscal extraction, could finance public goods? Consider the power of high-value Standards and Certifications. By creating regulatory frameworks that guarantee quality, safety, and international compatibility, the state creates a marketplace of Trust.

In this model, the state earns its revenue by providing an essential service: Gravity. When a government creates a regulatory environment so robust that it attracts international capital and domestic innovation, the fees associated with maintaining that environment become a voluntary exchange for market access.

  • Professional Licensing: Not as a barrier to entry, but as a guarantee of elite competence that commands premium prices.
  • Data Sovereignty Fees: Charging global platforms for the right to process indigenous data while ensuring that data is returned to the local economy as actionable intelligence.
  • Process Re-engineering Royalties: Where government-patented regulatory processes increase private sector efficiency, the state takes a small, performance-based dividend rather than a flat tax.

Baking the Cake vs. Wrapping the Cake

Most modern tax reform is focused on "wrapping the cake" — making the extraction process look prettier through digital portals and incentive schemes. But if the "cake" (the underlying economy) is shrinking or stagnant, no amount of decorative wrapping will generate sustainable revenue.

Plus Element advocates for Revenue Architecture. This involves shifting the burden away from the productive base (income and corporate activity) and toward the consumption of finite resources or the use of specific public infrastructure. Under this architecture:

1. The Prosperity Dividend

The state’s revenue increases *only* when the private sector’s productivity increases. By tying institutional income to the success of specific industries (like the Digital Economy), the government finally has a direct stake in removing the barriers to those industries' growth.

2. Asset-Backed Governance

Turning public data, indigenous content, and regulatory standards into digital assets that can be leveraged globally. This transforms the state from a "fiscal dependent" into a "sovereign originator" of value.

Strategic Outlook: The End of Extractive Logic

The challenge for the next generation of policy makers is not to find new things to tax, but to design new ways for the institution to be useful. In the age of AI and decentralized finance, the old mechanisms of seizure are failing. The future belongs to the Platform State — an institution that operates with the efficiency of a high-tech firm and the ethics of a public trust.

When utility is the driver of revenue, the conflict between the state and the citizen dissolves into a partnership of progress. Prosperity becomes the goal, and revenue becomes its natural byproduct.
