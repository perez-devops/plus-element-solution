The Formalisation Question: Can Regulatory Design Bring Nigeria's Informal Economy In from the Cold?

October 2025 9 min read

Nigeria's informal economy is not a "leakage" to be plugged; it is the resilient lifeblood of the nation's survival. Estimated to account for over 60% of GDP and the vast majority of employment, the informal sector is a testament to the Nigerian spirit of enterprise in the absence of institutional support. However, its continued informality represents a massive Strategic Deficit.

The quest for "formalisation" has long been dominated by the logic of extraction. Governments want to formalize the informal sector so they can tax it. But to the street trader, the artisan, or the micro-entrepreneur, formality currently looks like a zero-value proposition: more paperwork, more harassment, and more taxes, for no detectable increase in institutional utility.

"Informality is a rational response to a regulatory environment that makes visibility more expensive than productivity."

Formalisation as a Service, Not a Seizure

At Plus Element Solutions, we argue that the only durable way to formalize the economy is to change the Incentive Architecture. We must move from "Tax-First Formalisation" to "Utility-First Formalisation."

If formality provides a direct, measurable path to credit, to legal protection, to global market access, and to standardized quality, then entrepreneurs will formalize themselves. They won't need to be hunted; they will seek the Institutional Gravity of the state.

The Three Levers of Regulatory Conversion:

  • 1. Micro-Regulatory Sandboxes: Creating "low-friction" zones where micro-enterprises can operate legally with minimal reporting while they build the capacity for full compliance.
  • 2. Skills Certification as Collateral: Formalizing the apprenticeship system into a national skills certification framework. This turns "unrecognized labor" into a "bankable professional asset."
  • 3. Digital Equity Tokens: Using blockchain technology to give informal traders a digital identity that tracks their transactional history — effectively creating a "shadow credit score" that can be used to unlock formal financing.

The Cost of Invisibility

While informality allows for survival, its Cap on Growth is absolute. Informal businesses cannot scale because they cannot legally enter contracts with large corporations. They cannot export because they cannot meet international sanitary or quality standards. They cannot survive shocks because they have no access to insurance.

By leaving the majority of our people in the "cold" of informality, we are capping the nation's productivity at the level of the individual's hustle. Formalisation is about collective leverage. It is about building the institutional infrastructure that allows a thousand small hustles to become a dozen world-class industries.

A New Social Contract

The path forward requires a Institutional Reset. The state must prove its utility before it demands its tribute. This means simplifying the regulatory pathways to near-zero cost, providing "safety-net" regulations that protect the informal worker from exploitation, and ensuring that every naira of formal revenue is seen to be reinvested in the infrastructure that the informal sector actually uses.

The "Formalisation Question" is the defining challenge of Nigerian economic policy. The answer lies not in more enforcement, but in more Creative Design. When the benefits of being formal finally outweigh the costs of being invisible, the Nigerian economy will finally find its true temperature.
